Colonial "Parent" Country

sahistory.org.za
The European power overtook Rwanda through colonization and the implementation of divide-and-rule tactics. In 1884, at the Berlin Conference, European powers divided Africa into spheres of influence, leading to the colonization of Rwanda by Germany in 1894. After World War I, the League of Nations

granted Belgium control over Rwanda, which lasted until 1962. During this period, Belgium enforced a system of indirect rule, which utilized traditional Rwandan rulers to govern and created a hierarchy of power based on ethnicity. Also, the system favored the Tutsi ethnic group over the Hutus. This ultimately led to the 1994 Rwandan genocide. where approximately 800,000 Tutsis and moderate Hutus were killed by Hutu extremists.

The colonization of Rwanda by European powers, particularly Germany and Belgium, was driven by a desire for economic and political control of the region. Rwanda was rich in natural resources such as coffee, tea, tin, and other minerals. Additionally, the colonial powers sought to establish a foothold in the region to gain access to other markets in East Africa. The indigenous people of Rwanda were subjected to forced labor and heavy taxation, which served to enrich the colonial powers at the expense of the local population. The legacy of colonialism has had lasting effects on Rwanda and the wider region, including the ethnic tensions that led to the 1994 genocide.

Independence of Rwanda

Rwanda gained independence from Belgium on July 1, 1962. The process of independence was marked by a series of political upheavals and ethnic tensions between the majority Hutu and minority Tutsi groups. The independence movement was largely led by Hutu politicians who sought to end Belgian control and establish a democratic government that represented all Rwandans.

The path to independence was hastened by the Rwandan Revolution of 1959, in which Hutu politicians and activists overthrew the Tutsi monarchy and established a republic. This led to a period of violence and instability, with many Tutsi fleeing the country as refugees.

Belgium initially supported the Hutu-dominated government, but as the violence and discrimination against Tutsi continued, they began to withdraw their support. In 1961, they organized a referendum in which the majority of Rwandans voted in favor of independence.

newly-independent government was led by President Gregoire Kayibanda, a Hutu politician who had played a key role in the independence movement. However, ethnic tensions continued to simmer, and in 1994, Rwanda was plunged into a genocidal civil war that claimed the lives of an estimated 800,000 people, mostly Tutsi.

international community, including the United Nations and neighboring countries like Uganda and Tanzania, played a role in attempting to resolve the conflict and establish peace in the aftermath of the genocide. Today, Rwanda has made significant progress in rebuilding and reconciliation, and is considered one of the fastest-growing economies in Africa.

One of the most significant effects of imperialism in Rwanda was the creation of ethnic divisions between the Hutu and the Tutsi. The Belgian colonial government reinforced these divisions and gave preferential treatment to the Tutsi, leading to resentment among the Hutu. The imposition of colonial rule disrupted traditional Rwandan governance structures and created a power vacuum that ultimately led to political instability. This instability continues to manifest in Rwandan politics today.

Imperialism also caused hundreds of thousands of ethnic Tutsi Rwandans fled the country during the 1960s and remained refugees for more than 30 years. The chart above shows the rise of refugee numbers from 2000’s to this day. By the time Rwanda gained independence in 1962, around 200,000 Rwandan Tutsis had left to seek exile in neighboring states. Additionally, as many as 70,000 Tutsis fled into camps between 1959 and 1964 as Belgium transferred power to Hutu rulers

Other effects included forced labor, a refugee crisis, and increased local warfare. [3] The Hutu people lost their rights and were eliminated from positions of power, while the Tutsi people were educated, which created a divide between the two tribes. The culmination of these negative effects was the Rwandan Genocide of 1994, which resulted in the deaths of nearly a million people; Hutu extremists took over the government and began a campaign of violence against the Tutsi population, resulting in the deaths of hundreds of thousands of people. The effects of imperialism in Rwanda continue to be felt today, despite the country's economic growth and development.
